Everyday Code

Code affects nearly everything we do, and therefore everything we think, as it has built the most widely used infrastructure in the world. Code can be found in nearly everything we interact with today: We find entertainment in streaming and video apps, such as HBO Max and YouTube; social interaction in social media and networking apps, such as Instagram and LinkedIn; productivity in email and workplace apps, such as Gmail and Teams. As for my personal usage of applications, data collected from my IPhone over the entirety of last week concluded that I spend an average of 13 hours every day on my phone. This seems like an absurd amount, as it would mean that I would have to have my phone open for all waking hours of the day. Though in actuality, it is quite the opposite; A majority of my app usage occurs during the night. Many times I enjoy falling asleep to some form of noise, and for me that is almost always a YouTube video. This is likely why YouTube makes up 42% of my total phone usage. I also find myself on Instagram very often to scroll through Reels and send messages to friends and family.

Code and digital technology impact on education

Computer Science has entirely revolutionized modes of expression, and has led to a great distribution of wealth and knowledge across the globe ever since the World Wide Web was established. With the development of new coding langauges came exciting new outlets to express, design, and publish ideas in widely distributed digital environments. Through a more modern lens, these developments in coding have brought a polarizing, yet resourceful inlet of ideas to the world of composition: Artificial Intelligence in the form of Large Language Models (LLMs). These LLMs provide streams of information and ideas at the beck and call of users. This has led to a contentious rise in usage among many students and professors alike within a multitude of departments, with most contention likely arising from that of English departments. Due to the prediction-based nature of LLMs, many find that there is a degredation in the quality of information, as well as severe instances of plagiarism, in AI-produced texts; However, many others may find that LLM-produced texts improve the efficiency of composition, without noticing much of either of the aforementioned issues. With such differing positions, it is important to host and contribute to much discourse surrounding these products so that we may forge a path forward, whether it be a path of immutability, or a path of adaptation.
